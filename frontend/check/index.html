                Chaturanga            

           

                The Ancient Indian Strategy Game, Precursor to Modern Chess            

           

                Chaturanga, meaning "four limbs," is a two-player strategy game that originated in ancient India. It's a game of wit and foresight, reflecting the four divisions of a traditional army: infantry, cavalry, elephantry, and chariotry. Master the battlefield and checkmate your opponent's Raja.

The Pieces

           
                               
                   

Raja (King)

                   

Moves one square in any direction, just like the modern king. The objective is to protect your Raja and checkmate the opponent's.

               
                               
                   

Mantri (Minister)

                   

The precursor to the queen, this piece moves one square diagonally in any direction. Though limited, it is a crucial advisor to the Raja.

               
                               
                   

Gaja (Elephant)

                   

The equivalent of the bishop, the Gaja moves two squares diagonally, jumping over any piece in its path. It symbolizes the mighty war elephants.

               
                               
                   

Ashva (Horse)

                   

Moves in an L-shape, exactly like the modern knight, and can jump over other pieces. It represents the swift and agile cavalry.

               
                               
                   

Ratha (Chariot)

                   

Functions just like a modern rook, moving any number of squares horizontally or vertically. It symbolizes the powerful war chariots.

               
                               
                   

Padati (Foot Soldier)

                   

The pawns of Chaturanga. They move one square forward and capture diagonally, but unlike modern chess, they cannot move two squares on their first turn.
